# Client Proposal Template Kit
## Win More Projects with Professional, Persuasive Proposals

---

# QUICK-START GUIDE

## How to Use This Kit in Under an Hour

**Step 1 — Choose your template (2 minutes)**
Pick the template that matches your engagement type. The five options are: Web Design/Development, Marketing/SEO, Consulting, Creative/Branding, and SaaS/Technical Implementation.

**Step 2 — Fill in the client context (15 minutes)**
Read through the template once. Highlight every place marked with brackets like [CLIENT NAME] or [YOUR COMPANY]. Replace those with your specifics. Read the persuasion notes (marked with "> NOTE:") to understand what each section is doing.

**Step 3 — Set your pricing (10 minutes)**
Go to the Pricing section. Replace the placeholder numbers with your actual tiers. Use the anchoring guidance to position Good/Better/Best correctly.

**Step 4 — Personalize the problem statement (10 minutes)**
This is the highest-leverage section. Replace the generic problem description with specific language from your sales call or discovery conversation. Mirror the words the client used. This is what makes a proposal feel like it was written for them.

**Step 5 — Run the pre-send checklist (5 minutes)**
Find the Proposal Checklist at the end of this document. Go through it before you hit send. It takes five minutes and catches the mistakes that kill deals.

**Step 6 — Send and schedule your follow-ups**
Use one of the three follow-up email templates to schedule reminders in your calendar or task manager for Day 3, Day 7, and Day 14.

# CUSTOMIZATION GUIDE

## What to Always Change

These elements must be customized for every proposal. Missing any of them signals you're sending a template.

**Client-specific:**
- Client company name (every instance)
- Contact name and title
- Specific problems described (use language from your discovery call)
- Numbers and metrics mentioned (even estimates are better than blanks)
- Proposed start date and timeline
- Proposal expiration date

**Your information:**
- Your name, company, email, phone, website
- Your pricing (all three tiers)
- Your case studies and social proof
- Your availability and current project load

## What You Can Keep

The structure, section headers, and persuasion flow are battle-tested. You don't need to rewrite the architecture. The reasons the sections appear in this order are strategic.

**Keep:**
- The sequence (problem before solution, solution before pricing)
- The three-tier pricing structure
- The explicit Next Steps with numbered instructions
- The "Not Included" list in Scope
- The Terms in plain language

## How to Personalize Without Rewriting

**The 20% rule:** Change 20% of the proposal to be highly specific to the client and the conversation you had. The other 80% — structure, principles, general language — stays consistent. The 20% is what makes it feel custom.

**Mirror their language:** If they said "we're drowning in manual processes," use "drowning in manual processes" in your problem statement. If they called their tool their "tech stack," call it their tech stack. This is the fastest way to signal that you were paying attention.

**Reference the discovery call:** Mention the date you spoke, anything specific they said, anyone else on their team. These details are invisible to a prospect until they're missing — when they're there, proposals feel personal.

# APPENDIX: PERSUASION PRINCIPLES BEHIND THE STRUCTURE

Understanding *why* each section is where it is will help you adapt these templates intelligently when your situation is unusual.

**Why problem before solution?**
Clients need to feel understood before they feel sold to. A proposal that jumps straight to your services is about you. A proposal that first demonstrates that you've deeply understood their problem is about them. By the time you describe your solution, they're already nodding.

**Why three tiers?**
Single-price proposals turn every deal into a yes/no decision. Three-tier proposals turn every deal into a which-one decision. The middle tier does 70% of the conversion work — it feels like the sensible choice between the affordable option and the premium one. The "Best" tier also makes "Better" feel like a bargain, even if "Better" is exactly what you'd charge if you only had one price.

**Why explicit Next Steps?**
Decision-makers who like your proposal will still not act on it if the next step is ambiguous. The most common reason proposals go cold isn't that the client decided against you — it's that they meant to respond and then got distracted. A numbered, specific Next Steps section gives them a path back.

**Why "Not Included"?**
Scope ambiguity is the source of most client-consultant conflict. A clear "Not Included" list manages expectations before they become disputes, signals that you've done this before, and creates a natural framework for upselling additional services.

**Why follow up three times?**
Research on B2B sales consistently shows that the majority of deals close after the 5th contact, yet most salespeople give up after 1–2 follow-ups. Three structured, non-pushy follow-ups over two weeks hits the sweet spot between persistence and respect. The tone progression matters: warm check-in → value add → graceful close.
