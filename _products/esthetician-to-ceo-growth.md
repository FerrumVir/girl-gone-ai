### Price Increase Framework

**When to raise prices:**
- You're booked 2+ weeks out consistently
- You have a waitlist
- Your effective hourly rate is below $100
- You haven't raised prices in 6+ months
- You're turning away new clients

**How much to raise:**
- Moderate raise: 10-15% (safe, minimal client loss)
- Significant raise: 20-30% (expect 5-10% client turnover — usually the ones you're okay losing)
- Premium repositioning: 30-50% (requires upgraded experience/branding to match)

**Price Increase Script (for existing clients):**
```
"Hi [Name]! I wanted to let you know that starting [DATE —
4 weeks from now], my prices will be updating to reflect
[additional training/increased demand/enhanced
services/cost of doing business].

[SERVICE] will be $[NEW PRICE] (currently $[OLD PRICE]).

As a valued client, you're welcome to pre-book your next
[3] appointments at the current rate before [DATE].

Thank you for your continued trust — I'm always working
to bring you the best possible experience and results."
```

**Price Increase Timeline:**
1. Decide on new prices (4 weeks before)
2. Notify existing clients via text/email (3-4 weeks before)
3. Update website, booking page, all public materials (2 weeks before)
4. New prices take effect
5. Review: did you lose clients? How many? Net revenue change?

## PHASE 3: FIRST HIRE ($15K-$25K/month)

### Hiring Decision Framework

**Hire when:**
- You're losing money by NOT hiring (turning away revenue)
- You have systems documented (they need to follow something)
- You can afford 3 months of their pay as a safety net
- The hire will free you to do higher-value work OR generate net-new revenue

**What to hire first:**

| Role | When to Hire | Cost | Revenue Impact |
|------|-------------|------|----------------|
| Virtual Assistant | Drowning in admin (DMs, scheduling, emails) | $500-1,500/mo | Frees 8-15 hrs/week |
| Part-time esthetician | Turning away 10+ clients/week | $20-40/hr or commission | Adds $3,000-8,000/mo revenue |
| Social media manager | Posting inconsistently, no time for content | $500-2,000/mo | Indirect (growth) |
| Bookkeeper | Spending 5+ hrs/month on finances | $200-500/mo | Frees time + accuracy |
| Front desk/receptionist | 20+ clients/week, need check-in support | $15-20/hr PT | Improved client experience |

### Hiring Checklist (First Employee/Contractor)

- [ ] Determine: Employee (W-2) or Independent Contractor (1099)?
- [ ] Consult with accountant on classification rules
- [ ] Write clear job description with expectations
- [ ] Set compensation structure (hourly, commission, salary, hybrid)
- [ ] Create 30/60/90 day onboarding plan
- [ ] Document all SOPs they need to follow
- [ ] Set up payroll (Gusto, ADP, or manual)
- [ ] Verify insurance covers additional providers
- [ ] Non-compete/non-solicitation agreement (if applicable)
- [ ] Define success metrics (how do you evaluate performance?)
- [ ] Set schedule and communication expectations
- [ ] Training period plan (shadow you, supervised, then solo)

### Commission vs. Hourly (Esthetician Hire)

| Structure | Pros | Cons | Best For |
|-----------|------|------|----------|
| Hourly ($20-35/hr) | Predictable cost, simple | No incentive to build clientele | Part-time, task-based |
| Commission (40-60%) | Motivated to build book, aligned incentives | Unpredictable cost, slower start | Experienced provider |
| Base + Commission ($15/hr + 30-40%) | Security for them, incentive built in | More complex payroll | New-ish provider building book |
| Booth Rent ($200-600/week) | Predictable income for you, they run their business | Less control over quality/experience | Experienced, independent |

### Financial Model: Adding an Esthetician

```
SCENARIO: Hiring at 50% commission

New esthetician's revenue (Month 3 estimate): $5,000
Their commission (50%): -$2,500
Product cost for their services (15%): -$750
Additional overhead (supplies, laundry): -$200
YOUR NET from their work: $1,550/month

PLUS: Your time freed up to:
- Take on VIP/high-ticket clients only
- Develop passive income
- Work ON the business vs. IN it
- Reduce your hours without reducing income
```

## QUICK-START GUIDE

### Step 1: Know Your Numbers
Complete the Phase 1 assessment honestly. You can't improve what you don't measure. Calculate your ceiling, your effective hourly rate, and your capacity utilization.

### Step 2: Fix Your Pricing First
This is the highest-impact, lowest-effort change. If your effective hourly rate is under $100, raise prices before doing anything else.

### Step 3: Fill Your Capacity
Before adding complexity (hiring, passive income), maximize what you've already built. Get to 85% utilization with current capacity.

### Step 4: Build Systems
Document SOPs before hiring. Your first hire should walk into a clear system, not chaos. Automate everything that can be automated.

### Step 5: Hire Strategically
Hire to eliminate your lowest-value tasks first (admin), then hire to generate new revenue (another provider). Never hire from desperation.

### Step 6: Reduce Your Chair Time
As the business grows, spend less time doing services and more time leading, growing, and building. Your value as CEO exceeds your value as a solo provider.
