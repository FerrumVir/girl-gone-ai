## Market Rate Research Framework

### Step 1: Gather Data From Multiple Sources

**Salary Data Websites (check all 5):**

| Source | What It Offers | How to Use It |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| Glassdoor | Salary ranges by company, role, location | Search your exact title + target company |
| Levels.fyi | Tech compensation breakdowns (base + equity + bonus) | Best for tech/engineering roles |
| Payscale | Personalized salary report based on your details | Fill out the full profile for accuracy |
| LinkedIn Salary | Range by title, location, experience | Check "Salary Insights" on job postings |
| Bureau of Labor Statistics | National/regional averages by occupation | Good for baseline, less useful for negotiation |

**Additional Sources:**

- Company-specific: Blind (app), TeamBlind, company review sites
- Industry reports: Robert Half Salary Guide, Hays Salary Guide
- Recruiter conversations: Ask 2-3 recruiters "What's the range for this role?"
- Network: Ask peers in similar roles (use the script below)

### Step 3: Adjust for Your Specific Situation

**Factors that push you HIGHER in the range:**

- [ ] You have specialized skills that are hard to find
- [ ] You have more experience than the role requires
- [ ] The company is in a high cost-of-living area
- [ ] The role has been open for a long time (they're struggling to fill it)
- [ ] You have competing offers
- [ ] You bring unique industry knowledge or connections
- [ ] You have certifications or credentials others don't

**Factors that might keep you MID-range:**

- [ ] You meet the requirements but don't exceed them
- [ ] The company is a major brand (the name has value)
- [ ] The benefits package is exceptional
- [ ] There's significant growth potential

**Factors to consider if offered LOW range:**

- [ ] You're changing industries or roles (career pivot)
- [ ] You have less experience than they want
- [ ] It's a startup with equity (lower base, higher upside)
- [ ] The company is in a low cost-of-living area

### Step 4: Script for Asking Peers About Salary

```
"Hey [NAME], I'm doing market research for a role I'm considering/a raise I'm planning.
Would you be comfortable sharing a general range of what someone at your level
in [ROLE/INDUSTRY] is earning? I'm trying to calibrate my expectations.
Totally fine if you'd rather not — no pressure at all."
```

If they seem hesitant:
```
"Even a range (like 'between X and Y') would be super helpful.
And happy to share what I find from my research once it's compiled."
```

## Negotiation Scripts

### Script 1: When They Ask Your Salary Expectations (First)

**The Deflection (Best approach — delay naming a number):**

```
"I'd love to learn more about the full scope of the role before
discussing compensation. Could you share the budgeted range for this position?
That way I can confirm we're aligned before we go further."
```

**If they push:**

```
"Based on my research for this role in [LOCATION], market rate
is in the range of $[LOW] to $[HIGH]. My target would be
in the [MIDDLE-TO-HIGH END] of that range given my
[X YEARS OF EXPERIENCE / SPECIFIC SKILL / RELEVANT ACHIEVEMENT].
But I'm flexible — I want to understand the full compensation picture
including benefits, equity, and growth potential."
```

**If required to fill in a number on an application:**

Put the top of your research range. You can always come down. Never low-ball yourself in a form field.

---

### Script 2: Negotiating a New Job Offer (The Counter)

**When you receive an offer below your target:**

```
"Thank you so much for the offer — I'm genuinely excited about this role
and the team. I want to be transparent: based on my research and
experience, I was expecting something closer to $[YOUR TARGET].

Here's my thinking:
- My research shows market rate for this role in [LOCATION] is $[RANGE]
- I bring [SPECIFIC VALUE: X years in this exact space / a credential
  they need / I can hit the ground running with no ramp-up time]
- [If applicable: I have another offer at $X that I'm weighing]

Is there flexibility to move closer to $[YOUR TARGET]?"
```

---

### Script 3: Asking for a Raise (Current Employer)

**The Meeting Request:**

```
"[MANAGER], I'd like to schedule 30 minutes to discuss my compensation.
I've been reflecting on my contributions over the past [TIMEFRAME]
and I'd like to have a thoughtful conversation about aligning
my pay with the value I'm delivering. When works best for you?"
```

**The Conversation:**

```
"Thank you for making time for this. I want to start by saying
I'm committed to this role and this team. I'm not looking elsewhere —
I genuinely want to grow here.

That said, I want to discuss my compensation because I believe
there's a gap between what I'm contributing and what I'm earning.

Here's what I mean specifically:

1. When I started [X months/years ago], my role was [ORIGINAL SCOPE].
   Since then, I've taken on [EXPANDED RESPONSIBILITIES].

2. In the past [TIMEFRAME], I've delivered [ACHIEVEMENT 1 WITH METRIC],
   [ACHIEVEMENT 2 WITH METRIC], and [ACHIEVEMENT 3].

3. Based on market research, the range for someone in my role
   with my experience in [LOCATION] is $[RANGE].
   I'm currently at $[CURRENT], which puts me below market.

I'm requesting an adjustment to $[TARGET]. I believe this reflects
both my current contributions and the market rate for this work.

What are your thoughts?"
```

---

### Script 4: Negotiating Beyond Salary (Total Compensation)

**When base salary is firm but you want more:**

```
"I understand the base salary is [AMOUNT] and there isn't flexibility there.
I respect that. Can we discuss other elements of the package?

Here are a few things that would make this work for me:

- Signing bonus of $[AMOUNT] to bridge the gap
- Additional [X] days PTO (or flexible/unlimited PTO)
- Remote work [X] days per week
- Professional development budget of $[AMOUNT]/year
- Equity/stock options: [X shares or X% increase]
- Earlier performance review (at 6 months instead of 12)
  with a clear path to $[TARGET]
- Relocation assistance of $[AMOUNT]
- Title adjustment to [HIGHER TITLE]

I don't need all of these — but if we could work on [TOP 2-3],
I'd be thrilled to accept."
```

---

### Script 5: When They Give a Range and Ask If It Works

```
"Thank you for sharing that. The range of $[LOW] to $[HIGH]
is in the right ballpark. I'd be most comfortable at the [HIGHER END / AT $X]
given [SPECIFIC REASON: my experience level, the specialized skills I bring,
the current market for this expertise].

Can you tell me more about what determines where someone falls
within that range? I want to understand how decisions are made."
```

---

### Script 6: When You Have a Competing Offer

```
"I want to be transparent with you: I've received another offer
at $[AMOUNT OR 'a higher number']. I'm sharing this not as a pressure tactic —
I genuinely prefer [THIS COMPANY] because of [SPECIFIC REASON].

But I want to make a decision I won't second-guess.
Is there room to adjust the offer to $[TARGET]?
If we can get there, I'm ready to accept today."
```

**Important rules for using competing offers:**
- Only mention real offers (never bluff — it can backfire catastrophically)
- Express genuine preference for them (otherwise it just feels like a threat)
- Give them a way to "win" you — make the path clear

---

### Script 7: When They Say "We Don't Negotiate" or "That's Our Final Offer"

```
"I understand. I appreciate you being upfront about that.

Can I ask — is the base salary fixed, or is the entire package fixed?
Sometimes there's flexibility in [signing bonus / equity / PTO / title / start date /
review timeline] even when the base is set.

If the offer is truly final across the board, I respect that.
Let me take [X DAYS] to consider it carefully and I'll get back to you by [DATE]."
```

(NOTE: "We don't negotiate" is sometimes true and sometimes a test. Never react emotionally. Always stay gracious and curious.)

---

### Script 8: Negotiating After a Lowball Offer

```
"Thank you for the offer. I'm excited about the opportunity,
but I want to be honest: the number is significantly below
what I expected based on my research and experience.

I was targeting $[YOUR NUMBER] based on:
- Market rate for this role: $[RANGE FROM RESEARCH]
- My [X] years of directly relevant experience
- The fact that I'm currently earning $[CURRENT — only if it's higher]
- [SPECIFIC VALUE YOU BRING]

I understand budgets exist, and I'm not trying to be difficult.
But I need to see $[MINIMUM YOU'D ACCEPT] to make this work.
What can we do?"
```

---

### Script 9: Negotiating a Promotion (Internal Move)

```
"I'm thrilled about the promotion to [NEW TITLE]. Thank you for
recognizing my contributions. I want to discuss the compensation
that comes with this new role.

The increase from $[CURRENT] to $[OFFERED] is a [X]% raise.
While I appreciate it, the market rate for a [NEW TITLE] with my
experience is $[MARKET RATE]. That's a [$X] gap.

I understand that internal promotions sometimes come with smaller
increases than external hires would receive. But I'd like to find
a middle ground — perhaps $[YOUR TARGET] now, with a clear path
to $[MARKET RATE] at my next review in [TIMEFRAME]?

I want to make sure I'm set up to fully commit to this new level
of responsibility without feeling undervalued."
```

---

### Script 10: Following Up When They Need "Approval"

```
"Hi [NAME], I wanted to check in on the compensation discussion.
I know you mentioned needing to get approval from [FINANCE/LEADERSHIP/HR].

I remain very enthusiastic about the role — it's my top choice.
Is there any additional information I can provide that would help
make the case for $[YOUR ASK]?

Happy to provide [market data / a summary of my relevant experience /
reference contact information] if that would be useful in the conversation."
```

---

### Script 11: Accepting an Offer (After Successful Negotiation)

```
"Thank you so much. I'm thrilled to accept the offer of $[FINAL AMOUNT]
[plus the agreed-upon BONUS/EQUITY/BENEFITS].

Could you send me the updated offer letter reflecting our discussion?
Once I have it in writing, I'll sign and return it within [TIMEFRAME].

I'm excited to join the team and I appreciate your willingness
to work with me on this. Looking forward to starting on [DATE]."
```

---

### Script 12: Declining an Offer Gracefully

```
"Thank you for the offer and for the time you've invested in this process.
After careful consideration, I've decided to [accept another opportunity /
stay in my current role / go in a different direction].

This wasn't an easy decision — I have a lot of respect for [COMPANY]
and the team. The compensation gap was ultimately the deciding factor,
and I understand that you offered your best.

I hope we can stay in touch. If circumstances change on either side,
I'd love to revisit this conversation in the future."
```

## Counter-Offer Templates

### Email Counter-Offer Template 1: Professional & Direct

**Subject:** Re: [JOB TITLE] Offer — Compensation Discussion

---

Hi [HIRING MANAGER],

Thank you for the offer to join [COMPANY] as [TITLE]. I'm genuinely excited about this opportunity and the team you're building.

After reviewing the details, I'd like to discuss the compensation:

**Your offer:** $[AMOUNT] base + [OTHER COMPONENTS]
**My request:** $[TARGET] base + [ANY ADDITIONAL REQUESTS]

**My reasoning:**
- Market data from [2-3 SOURCES] shows the range for this role in [LOCATION] is $[RANGE]
- I bring [SPECIFIC QUALIFIER: X years, specific skill, certification, immediate impact capability]
- [Optional: I'm currently earning $X / have another offer at $X]

I'm not trying to play games — this is a number I've arrived at through research, and it's what I need to accept with full enthusiasm and commitment.

Is there room to move closer to my ask? I'm open to creative solutions (signing bonus, equity, review timeline) if the base has a hard cap.

Looking forward to finding a number that works for both of us.

Best,
[Your Name]

---

### Email Counter-Offer Template 2: Soft & Collaborative

**Subject:** Re: Offer Letter — A few thoughts

---

Hi [NAME],

I'm so excited about this offer. The role, the team, the mission — it all aligns perfectly with where I want to take my career.

I'd love to accept right away, but I want to be thoughtful. After reviewing the compensation and doing some market research, I'm hoping we can explore a small adjustment.

Would it be possible to bring the base salary to $[TARGET]? This is based on [BRIEF REASONING: market rate for this role + my relevant experience].

I want to be somewhere I'm fully committed and not second-guessing — and getting to $[TARGET] would put me there completely.

Totally open to a call to discuss. What works for you?

[Your Name]

### Verbal Counter-Offer Framework

```
Step 1 — THANK: "Thank you for this offer. I'm excited about it."
Step 2 — BRIDGE: "I'd like to discuss one thing before I accept."
Step 3 — STATE: "Based on my research, I'm targeting $[X]."
Step 4 — JUSTIFY: "Here's why: [1-2 brief reasons]."
Step 5 — ASK: "Is there flexibility to get there?"
Step 6 — SILENCE: Stop talking. Let them respond.
```

## Confidence Exercises

### Exercise 1: The Mirror Practice (5 Minutes)

Stand in front of a mirror. Say these phrases out loud, maintaining eye contact with yourself:

1. "Based on my research, I'm targeting $[YOUR NUMBER]."
2. "I bring [SPECIFIC VALUE] that justifies this ask."
3. "Is there flexibility in the offer?"
4. "I need to see $[NUMBER] to accept with full commitment."
5. "What can we do to bridge the gap?"

Repeat each one 3 times. Notice your body language. Stand tall. Speak at a measured pace. Don't rush.

**Why this works:** Negotiation anxiety often comes from never having said the words out loud. Familiarity breeds confidence.

---

### Exercise 2: The Worst-Case Scenario (Journaling)

Write answers to these questions:

1. "What's the absolute worst thing that could happen if I negotiate?"
   Answer: _________________________________

2. "Has anyone ever had a job offer rescinded just because they negotiated?"
   Answer: Virtually never. Reasonable negotiation is expected.

3. "If they say no, what actually happens?"
   Answer: I still have the original offer. Nothing is lost.

4. "What's the worst thing that happens if I DON'T negotiate?"
   Answer: I leave money on the table for years. Compound that over a career.

5. "What would I tell my best friend to do in this situation?"
   Answer: _________________________________

---

### Exercise 3: The Lifetime Cost Calculator

Calculate what NOT negotiating costs you:

```
Offered salary:              $__________
Your target (after negotiation): $__________
Difference:                  $__________

That difference x 1 year:    $__________
That difference x 5 years:   $__________ (with ~3% annual raises on a higher base)
That difference x 10 years:  $__________
That difference x career:    $__________ (often $500K-$1M+)
```

Plus: future raises are calculated as percentages of your base. A higher starting point means every future raise is larger.

**One 15-minute conversation can be worth $100,000+ over your career.**

---

### Exercise 4: Power Poses (2 Minutes Before the Conversation)

Before the call or meeting:
- Stand in a private space (bathroom works)
- Feet shoulder-width apart, hands on hips (Wonder Woman pose)
- Hold for 2 minutes
- Breathe deeply and slowly
- Visualize the conversation going well
- Remind yourself: "This is a normal business conversation. They expect it."

---

### Exercise 5: Reframe Your Internal Narrative

Replace these thoughts:

| Anxious Thought | Reframe |
|----------------|---------|
| "I'm being greedy" | "I'm advocating for fair market value" |
| "They'll think I'm difficult" | "They'll think I'm informed and professional" |
| "What if they take the offer back?" | "Companies don't rescind offers over reasonable negotiation" |
| "I should just be grateful" | "Gratitude and fair pay aren't mutually exclusive" |
| "I don't want to seem pushy" | "Asking once, respectfully, is not pushy" |
| "I'm not worth that much" | "The market data says I am. Trust the data." |

## Common Objections & Responses

### "We don't have budget for that."

```
"I understand budget constraints are real. A few options:
- Could we do a signing bonus to bridge the gap?
- What about an accelerated review at 6 months with a clear path to my target?
- Is there flexibility in equity, PTO, or professional development budget?
I'm flexible on HOW we get there — the total value is what matters to me."
```

### "That's above the range for this role."

```
"I appreciate you sharing that. Can you tell me what the range is?
If my target is above range, I'd love to understand what would need to be true
for someone to reach that level — whether it's a title adjustment,
an accelerated path, or additional scope I could take on."
```

### "We pay everyone at the same level equally."

```
"I respect pay equity — it's important to me too.
Can you help me understand what determines where someone falls
within the band? If there are criteria I meet that would justify
the higher end, I'd like to make that case."
```

### "Your experience doesn't match what we'd normally pay at that level."

```
"Fair point. Here's what I'd offer as context:
While my years of experience might be [less than typical],
my [SPECIFIC RESULTS / SPECIALIZED SKILL / UNIQUE BACKGROUND]
means I can deliver at the level of someone with more years.
Would you be open to a performance-based path — start at $[SLIGHTLY LOWER],
with a guaranteed raise to $[TARGET] after 6 months if I hit [SPECIFIC METRICS]?"
```

### "We can revisit this at your annual review."

```
"I appreciate that. To make sure we're aligned:
Can we agree on specific metrics that would trigger the increase?
And can we put the target number ($[X]) in writing now,
so it's a concrete goal rather than an open question at review time?
I want to make sure we both know what success looks like."
```

### "The benefits package makes up for the lower salary."

```
"I've looked at the benefits package and it's strong — I appreciate that.
That said, when I benchmark total compensation (base + benefits + equity),
I'm still seeing a gap of approximately $[AMOUNT] versus market rate.
Could we close that gap with [SPECIFIC ASK: higher base, signing bonus, more equity]?"
```
